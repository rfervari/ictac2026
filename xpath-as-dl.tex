\section{From $\xpath$ to $\alcd$}
\label{sec:xp-as-alcd}

In this section we show how to characterize $\xpath$ as a fragment of $\alcd$.
The connection between the Boolean components is straightforward. Path expressions of $\xpath$ can be naturally translated as abstract paths.
The interesting element in the translation is the use of concrete features to encode data values and the inclusion of an equality predicate in the concrete domain as a way to encode data comparisons. It turns out that to map $\xpath$ as a fragment of $\alcd$ we need very simple (admissible) concrete domains of the form $\cdom = \tup{\D, \{= , \neq, \botpred, \toppred\}}$; where $\D$ captures the universe of data values, and $=$ and $\neq$ capture equality and inequality on $\D$.
We refer to these simple concrete domains as \emph{equality domains}.
The translation from $\xpath$ expressions to $\alcd$ concepts is given next.

\begin{definition} \label{def:translation} 
    The \emph{translation function} $\st$ from node expressions of $\xpath$ into concepts of $\alcd$ is defined recursively as:
    \begin{align*}
        \st(\apath_i) &=f_i &   \st(p_i) &= C_i\\
        \st(\alpha\beta) &= \st(\alpha)\st(\beta) &       
        \st(\lnot \varphi) &= \lnot \st(\varphi) \\
        \st(\tup{\alpha =_{\cmp_i}    \beta})&= \exists \st(\alpha)g_i, \st(\beta)g_i.{=}  &
        \st(\varphi \land \psi) &= \st(\varphi) \sqcap \st(\psi) \\
        \st(\tup{\alpha \neq_{\cmp_i}   \beta})&= \exists \st(\alpha)g_i, \st(\beta)g_i.{\neq} 
    \end{align*}
\end{definition}

To show correctness of the translation, we need to put models of $\xpath$ and interpretations from~$\alcd$ in correspondence.

\begin{definition} \label{def:model:as:interpretation}
    Let $\model= \tup{ \N, \D, \set{\R_{\apath}}_{\apath \in \mods}, \valuation,\set{\data_\cmp}_{\cmp \in \cmps} }$ be a data model. 
    The equality domain $\cdom^{\model} = \tup{\D, \set{=, \neq,\botpred, \toppred}}$ is s.t.\
        the extension of $=$ is equality on $\D$,
        the extension of $\neq$ is inequality on $\D$,
        the extension of $\botpred$ is $\emptyset$, and
        the extension of $\toppred$ is $\D$.
    Then, define $\interp_\model = \tup{ \N, {\cdot^{\interp_\model}}}$ where: 
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item 
            ${C}_{i}^{\interp_\model} = \truthset{p_i}{\model}$
        \item 
            ${r_i}^{\interp_\model} = \emptyset$
        \item 
            ${f}_{i}^{\interp_\model} = \truthset{\apath_i}{\model}$ 
        \item 
            ${g}_{i}^{\interp_\model}(n) = \data_{\cmp_i}(n)$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The following result states that data models of $\xpath$ can be seen as interpretations of $\alcd$ where $\cdom$ is an equality domain.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma:models-xp-dl}
    $\interp_\model$ is a interpretation of $\alcd$ and $\cdom^\model$ is an admissible concrete domain.
\end{lemma}

The proof of the lemma above is straightforward, and enables us to establish the following correspondence between the two logics. 

\begin{theorem}\label{th:translation-correctness}
    Let $\varphi$ be a node expression, $\alpha$ a path expression, and $\model$ a data model, then $\truthset{\alpha}{\model}=\st(\alpha)^{\interp_\model}$ and $\truthset{\varphi}{\model}=\st(\varphi)^{\interp_\model}$. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Let $\model = \tup{ \N, \D, \set{\R_{\apath}}_{\apath \in \mods}, \valuation,\set{\data_\cmp}_{\cmp \in \cmps} }$, and $n \in \N$.
    The proof is by induction on $\varphi$ and $\alpha$. Base cases and Boolean inductive cases are direct by definition. We show below the case for $=_\cmp$ data comparisons (the case of $\neq_\cmp$ being analogous).
    \begin{description}
        \item[($\varphi = \tup{\alpha =_{\cmp_i} \beta}$).] 
                
        We prove only the left-to-right direction. The right-to-left direction is analogous.
        Let ${n \in \truthset{  \tup{\alpha =_{\cmp_i} \beta}}{\model}}$. 
        By \Cref{def:xpath:semantics}, there are $x$ and $y$ s.t.\
            $(n,x) \in \truthset{ \alpha}{\model}$,
            $(n,y) \in  \truthset{ \beta}{\model}$, and
            $\data_{\cmp_i}(x) = \data_{\cmp_i}(y)$. 
        By \Cref{def:model:as:interpretation}, we know
            (1) $(x,\data_{\cmp_i}(x)) \in (g_i)^{\interp_\model}$ and
            (2) ${(y,\data_{\cmp_i}(y)) \in (g_i)^{\interp_\model}}$.
        From the IH,
            (3) ${(n,x) \in \st(\alpha)^{\interp_\model}}$ and
            (4) $(n,y) \in \st(\beta)^{\interp_\model}$. 
        Using (1) and (3),
            we obtain $(n,\data_{\cmp_i}(x)) \in (\st(\alpha) g_i)^{\interp_\model}$. 
        Using (2) and (4),
            we obtain  $(n,\data_{\cmp_i}(y)) \in (\st(\beta) g_i)^{\interp_\model}$.
        Therefore, $n \in (\exists \st(\alpha)g_i, \st(\beta)g_i. =)^{\interp_\model}$.
        Finally, by \Cref{def:translation}, $n \in \st(\tup{\alpha =_{\cmp_i} \beta})^{\interp_\model}$. \qed 
    \end{description}

The result in \Cref{th:translation-correctness} tells us that $\xpath$ forms a very simple fragment of $\alcd$.
This result further makes it possible to define and study the properties of standard reasoning methods for $\xpath$ in a DL setting. In particular, we obtain complexity upper bounds for them, by~\Cref{prop:lutz}. However these bounds are not optimal. The next section is devoted to show tight complexity bounds for $\xpath$.
